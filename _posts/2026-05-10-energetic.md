Since moving to NYC 5 months ago, I’ve gotten really into this board game.
![collage](/assets/images/energetic/collage.png)

The name of the game is Energetic. It’s a clean-energy-themed collaborative board game where the goal is to build 16 Gigawatts of clean energy by the year 2035. Each year, you draw action cards and spend money to place infrastructure tokens, such as solar, wind, and transmission, on the board. At the same time, you have to manage (a) the budget, (b) the grid stability, and (c) public opinion– all of which relate to one another. For instance, if you build unpopular infrastructure that tanks your public opinion, you might lose the next election and get your budget cut in half!

The game helps you knit together disparate concepts into a cohesive picture. Like, even if you read a lot of news stories about wind or solar or whatever, you may not have a foundational intuition of how the pieces of the puzzle fit together. *If we build this, how does it affect that? If we do that, how does it affect this other thing?* What are our options? What are our constraints? What are our strategies?

Like, how do solar and wind and nuclear and hydro stack up against each other?
![infra_cards](/assets/images/energetic/infra_cards.png)

What research and policy levers can we pull (or not pull)?
![research_policy_cards](/assets/images/energetic/research_policy_cards.png)

And how do we wrap our heads around the scale of it all? We know we have to build 16 Gigawatts, but what is a Gigawatt, really? What does a Gigawatt _feel_ like?
![solar_covering_manhattan](/assets/images/energetic/solar_covering_manhattan.png)

16 Gigawatts divided by 8 million New Yorkers is 2 kilowatts per person. The UN defines 2 kW as "a sustainable level of power consumption." This may seem low, since today's middle-class US citizen uses 4 kW on average. (In NYC it's actually lower, because people live in dense apartments and take public transit.) But even 16 GW is a conservative estimate that "assumes a significant reduction in power demand through behavioral change and energy efficiency."[^1]

I once spent a day installing solar panels on some guy's roof with 3 other people. It took us 6 hours to install 10 panels, which is like 3 kilowatts. Thinking about that day out in the sun, and then looking at the diagram above, I feel the diagram more viscerally.
![aldhar_solar](/assets/images/energetic/aldhar_solar.jpg)



And at its best, the game gives you a taste of systems thinking [kirpichov]. In a world where AI can do all your homework assignments, systems thinking remains a uniquely human skill (at least for now)-- one that's sorely neglected by the K12 system. The real world doesn’t look like homework assignments; it looks like navigating uncertainty and collaborating and making mistakes while you try to solve problems that nobody knows how to solve.

The game has a steep learning curve as you stumble through the first few turns, but by the second and third hour of play, you’re off and running and having a great time. It mirrors real life in that way– initial progress seems excruciatingly slow and your targets seem hopelessly out of reach, but if you invest early in research + policy + infrastructure, the benefits compound over time and kick off a virtuous cycle. For example, if you play multiple infrastructure tokens in a region, you unlock a “cluster benefit”, which improves the public-opinion impact of future infrastructure. As you unlock more and more benefits, you begin to see an exponential takeoff on the board. But at the same time, your problems get exponentially worse– natural disasters, market crashes, pandemics (all represented by red “random event” cards which increase in frequency after the year 2031). It feels both incredibly pessimistic and incredibly optimistic, which is both thrilling and head-spinning. You get the sensation of stepping outside of yourself and confronting what the city and the world will look like over the next 30 years. Even when refracted through the lens of a board game, the size and scale of it are awesome to behold.

I’d like to talk some more about this feeling of being part of something larger than yourself. It’s intoxicating. You let that feeling into your life and you want more and more of it.

There's this short story, “The Dome”, in which aliens place an impenetrable transparent dome over a suburban town (for shits and giggles), and we witness the rapid breakdown of social structure as the town descends into panic. The climate crisis is likely going to make us feel like that. The Earth is our Dome. We are at its mercy. We do not yet know how to control it; we do not yet know how to escape it. If we raise global temperatures by 5℃, it is we who will perish, not the Earth. The Earth is much bigger and older and wiser than us.

Sometimes you look up at the open sky and think about how little control you have over it all. With the release of control comes the release of a certain kind of anxiety that Thoreau described as a “quiet desperation”. [Quote]
When I heard this phrase a few weeks ago, it really struck me, because I’ve often felt like I’ve been living in quiet desperation. I’ve often been too focused on myself– my own grades, my extracurriculars, my career stability, my various physical and emotional needs.
I’m a grinder. I grind because I worry all night.. All the threats to me and to, um, my division and my physical body. https://youtu.be/bmj0RA9QLJc?si=eYB8ALjo13sZxDx-

We cannot grind our way out of the climate crisis. We need a shift in public opinion. It’s the key that unlocks the whole puzzle, but it's the hardest to change.

Anyone who’s played the game will have the most intense love-hate relationship with public opinion.
. The reality per Vivas Kumar is that climate is a niche subtopic. It’s not that motivating of a factor outside of the (sorry) “liberal bubble”. It may motivate choices on the margin (paper vs plastic straws), but it doesn’t fundamentally shift how people decide to buy, invest, and pursue economic stability.

So how does one concoct the feeling of being outside oneself? A psychedelic trip. The birth of a child. A close brush with death. Looking at the stars on a cloudless night. Or, as Ryan Kemp put it, 
The joyful struggle of touching something real.
I’d like to invite more people into this feeling. I see this as an awesome and challenging endeavour– one that is big enough to encompass my whole life and the lives of many others. (like TempleOS)

I hope to use this game not as a way to teach people, but to connect with people. To find people to join in swimming together like salmon up a massive stream. In the next month or so, I would love nothing more than to gather a group of friends from different parts of my life, introduce them to each other, and enjoy a nice board game over afternoon tea and snacks.
